Methane-oxidizing microbial consortia

Huge amounts of methane, a potent greenhouse gas, are prevented from reaching the atmosphere by the action of microbial consortia living in the ocean sediments. The symbiotic relationship between the two microbial partners in these consortia, which probably involves the exchange of metabolites between partners, is poorly understood. I tested for a relationship between carbon metabolism and the hydrogen isotopic composition (Î´D) of lipids in one of the microbial partners, which could shed light on the nature of the metabolites exchanged between the partners.

The problem

Up to 25% of the gobal production of methane occurs in ocean sediments. Before it even leaves the sediments, more than 90% of this methane is consumed by a symbiotic process between methane-oxidizing archaea and sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB). The SRB are thought to take up the waste products (metabolites) of the archaea, allowing the archaea to continue consuming methane. This likely explains why the SRB are often found in a layer around a clump of archaea, as shown in the bottom right corner of the figure. After years of scientific study, the nature of the metabolites passed between the archaea and the SRB is still unknown.
