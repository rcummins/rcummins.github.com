Earth science outreach activities

I've tried these activities with many classes of middle school students, and they were all a big hit.

Pangaea puzzle

The plates have moved around a lot since 250 million years ago when the supercontinent Pangaea started to break up. This puzzle includes all the major continental plates in the southern hemisphere. Using the shapes of the continents and evidence from land-dwelling fossils whose ranges cover multiple continents, students put together the pieces to recreate Pangaea. Puzzle materials can be found here. A great movie to show after the students finish, titled "Continental drift", can be found here.

Fossil identification

For this activity, I first have the students go through and try to name the organism in each of the pictures. I ask about important differences between similar organisms, like snails and ammonites or the two types of plants. Then I pass out boxes of numbered fossils, one fossil for each picture. The students get to pick up the fossils and look at them, and try to figure out which organism they represent. The students are always excited about getting to hold real fossils in their hands.

Relative dating

Fossils are still widely used by geologists to date rocks relative to each other. I first show this diagram to the students, and explain that the horizontal lines represent the times when these different organisms were living. Then I ask for volunteers to hold pieces of paper that represent rocks. Each piece of paper has two fossils printed on it, and the volunteers stand at the front of the classroom holding the paper so the rest of the class can see it. Then the class works together to put the "rocks" in order from oldest to youngest. I include a few papers with fossils from overlapping times, so that the relative age of the "rocks" is ambiguous. This usually frustrates the students, because there is no right answer, but this ambiguity is often the case in real science, so it's an important lesson.
